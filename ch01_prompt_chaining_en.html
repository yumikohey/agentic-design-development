What is Prompt Chaining?

Prompt Chaining — also known as the Pipeline Pattern — is a powerful paradigm for handling complex tasks with large language models (LLMs). Rather than expecting an LLM to solve a complex problem in a single monolithic step, prompt chaining advocates for a divide-and-conquer strategy. The core idea is to break down the original problem into a sequence of smaller, more manageable sub-problems, each addressed by a specifically designed prompt.

Advantages of Sequential Processing

This sequential processing technique inherently introduces modularity and clarity. By decomposing a complex task, it becomes easier to understand and debug each individual step, making the overall process more robust and interpretable. Each step in the chain can be meticulously crafted and optimized to focus on a specific aspect of the larger problem, leading to more accurate and focused outputs.

Integrating External Tools

Prompt chaining is not just about breaking down problems — it also enables the integration of external knowledge and tools. At each step, the LLM can be instructed to interact with external systems, APIs, or databases, enriching its knowledge and abilities beyond its internal training data. This dramatically expands the potential of LLMs, allowing them to function not just as isolated models but as integral components of broader, more intelligent systems.

Foundation for Building AI Agents

The significance of prompt chaining extends beyond simple problem-solving. It serves as a foundational technique for building sophisticated AI agents. These agents can utilize prompt chains to autonomously plan, reason, and act in dynamic environments. By strategically structuring the sequence of prompts, an agent can engage in tasks requiring multi-step reasoning, planning, and decision-making, more closely mirroring human thought processes.

Limitations of Single Prompts
For multifaceted tasks, using a single complex prompt causes the model to struggle with constraints and instructions. Click each card to reveal the failure mode in detail:

Instruction Neglect

Parts of the prompt are silently ignored

When a prompt contains too many competing instructions, the model selectively ignores portions. The more complex the prompt, the more content gets skipped, resulting in incomplete or misaligned outputs.

Contextual Drift

Model loses track of the initial context

As prompts grow longer and more complex, the model progressively drifts away from the original goals. Later outputs may contradict or ignore earlier requirements in the same prompt.

Error Propagation

Early mistakes compound and amplify

In a single prompt, once the model makes an early error, that error gets amplified by subsequent reasoning steps. A small misinterpretation early on can snowball into a significantly incorrect final answer.

Context Window Limits

Insufficient information degrades response quality

When a prompt requires extensive input data, context window limits prevent the model from accessing all necessary information simultaneously, forcing it to work with an incomplete picture.

Hallucination

Cognitive overload generates incorrect facts

When the model simultaneously handles too many tasks, the increased cognitive load significantly raises the probability of generating factually incorrect information — a phenomenon known as hallucination.

Real-World Failure Example

A single query asks the model to: analyze a market research report → summarize findings → identify trends with data points → draft an email. The model might summarize well but fail to extract data or compose the email correctly. When multiple objectives compete for attention, overall quality degrades sharply.

Sequential Decomposition: The Chain Solution
Prompt chaining addresses these challenges by breaking the complex task into a focused, sequential workflow. Click each step to see the details:
1
Summarize the Report
Role: Market Analyst
Prompt: "Summarize the key findings of the following market research report: [text]."

The model's sole focus is summarization, greatly increasing the accuracy of this initial step. By constraining the task to a single operation, the model can devote its full capacity to content understanding and summary generation.

Input: Raw market research report
Output: Concise summary of key findings
2
Identify Trends & Data
Role: Trade Analyst
Prompt: "Using the summary, identify the top three emerging trends and extract the specific data points that support each trend: [output from step 1]."

This prompt is more constrained and builds directly on a validated output. The model no longer needs to understand the full raw report — it works from the already-distilled summary to perform targeted extraction.

Input: Summary from Step 1
Output: Trend list with supporting data (ideally structured JSON)
3
Compose the Email
Role: Expert Documentation Writer
Prompt: "Draft a concise email to the marketing team outlining the following trends and their supporting data: [output from step 2]."

The final step composes a professional email from clean, structured data. Each step reduces ambiguity and cognitive load. Assigning distinct roles ensures accurate, domain-appropriate output at every stage.

Input: JSON trend data from Step 2
Output: Professional email to the marketing team

Why This Works

This decomposition allows for more granular control over the process. Each step is simpler and less ambiguous, reducing the model's cognitive load and leading to more accurate and reliable final output. This modularity is analogous to a computational pipeline — each function performs a specific operation before passing its result to the next.

The Role of Structured Output
The reliability of a prompt chain is highly dependent on the integrity of the data passed between steps. If the output of one prompt is ambiguous or poorly formatted, the subsequent prompt may fail due to faulty input.

Why Structured Formats Are Critical

Specifying structured output formats — such as JSON or XML — is crucial. This practice minimizes errors that can arise from interpreting natural language, and is a key component in building robust, multi-step LLM systems. Structured formats ensure data is machine-readable, precisely parseable, and can be inserted unambiguously into the next prompt.

For example, the output from the trend identification step can be formatted as a JSON object:

{ "trends": [ { "trend_name": "AI-Powered Personalization", "supporting_data": "73% of consumers prefer to do business with brands that use personal information to make their shopping experiences more relevant." }, { "trend_name": "Sustainable and Ethical Brands", "supporting_data": "Sales of products with ESG-related claims grew 28% over the last five years, compared to 20% for products without." } ] }

Benefits of Structured Output

  • Data is precisely parsed — no ambiguity in handoff between steps
  • Reduces errors arising from natural language interpretation
  • Clear input/output contracts for each step in the chain
  • Enables validation and transformation logic between steps
  • Supports deterministic downstream processing and branching

Role Assignment in Chains

Assigning distinct roles to each step (e.g., "Market Analyst," "Trade Analyst," "Expert Documentation Writer") narrows the model's focus at each stage. This is a design pattern — not a technical requirement — that produces more accurate, domain-appropriate output by constraining the model's task scope per step.

Practical Applications & Use Cases
Prompt chaining is a versatile pattern applicable across a wide range of scenarios when building agentic systems. Click the tabs to explore seven key applications:

Information Processing Workflows

Many tasks involve processing raw information through multiple transformations — summarizing a document, extracting key entities, then using those entities to query a database or generate a report.

1
Extract text content from a given URL or document
2
Summarize the cleaned text
3
Extract specific entities (names, dates, locations) from the summary
4
Use the entities to search an internal knowledge base
5
Generate a final report incorporating the summary, entities, and search results

Applied in automated content analysis, AI-driven research assistants, and complex report generation systems.

Complex Query Answering

Complex questions requiring multiple steps of reasoning or information retrieval. For example: "What were the main causes of the stock market crash in 1929, and how did government policy respond?"

1
Identify the core sub-questions in the user's query (causes of crash, government response)
2
Research or retrieve information specifically about the 1929 crash causes
3
Research or retrieve information about the government's policy response
4
Synthesize information from steps 2 and 3 into a coherent answer

Complex operations often combine parallel processing (independent data gathering) with prompt chaining (dependent synthesis and refinement steps).

Data Extraction & Transformation

Iteratively converting unstructured text into structured formats, supporting conditional branching and external tool calls.

1
Attempt to extract specific fields (name, address, amount) from an invoice document
⚙
Validate: check if all required fields were extracted and meet format requirements
2
Conditional prompt: if fields are missing or malformed, craft a new prompt to find the missing info
⚙
Validate again and repeat if necessary
✓
Output validated, structured data

Content Generation Workflows

Complex content creation decomposed into ideation, outlining, drafting, and revision phases.

1
Generate 5 topic ideas based on a user's general interest
⚙
User selects or system auto-selects the best topic
2
Based on the selected topic, generate a detailed outline
3
Write a draft section for each outline point (providing prior section as context)
4
Review and refine the complete draft for coherence, tone, and grammar

Applied for creative narratives, technical documentation, and other structured text content.

Conversational Agents with State

Prompt chaining provides the foundational mechanism for preserving conversational continuity by constructing each dialogue turn as a new prompt that systematically incorporates information from preceding interactions.

1
Process user utterance, identify intent and key entities
⚙
Update conversation state with intent and entities
2
Generate a response based on current state, or identify the next needed piece of information
↻
Repeat for subsequent turns — each new input leverages the accumulating conversation history

Code Generation & Refinement

Decomposing coding tasks into progressive stages, allowing insertion of deterministic logic between model calls.

1
Understand the user's request, generate pseudocode or an outline
2
Write an initial code draft based on the outline
3
Identify potential errors or areas for improvement (static analysis or another LLM call)
4
Rewrite or refine the code based on the identified issues
5
Add documentation and test cases

Multimodal & Multi-Step Reasoning

Analyzing datasets with diverse modalities (text, image, audio) requires breaking the problem into prompt-based sub-tasks. For example, interpreting an image with embedded text, annotation labels, and tabular data:

1
Extract and comprehend the text from the user's image request
2
Link the extracted image text with its corresponding labels
3
Use the tabular information to interpret the gathered information and determine the required output
Hands-On Code
The following code uses LangChain to implement a two-step prompt chain as a data processing pipeline. The first step extracts information from unstructured text; the second transforms the extracted result into structured JSON.

Available Frameworks

Frameworks for implementing prompt chains include LangChain, LangGraph, Crew AI, and Google's Agent Development Kit (ADK). LangChain provides foundational abstractions for linear sequences. LangGraph extends these capabilities to support stateful and cyclical computations needed for more sophisticated agentic behaviors.

Install dependencies:

pip install langchain langchain-community langchain-openai langgraph

Full implementation:

import os from langchain_openai import ChatOpenAI from langchain_core.prompts import ChatPromptTemplate from langchain_core.output_parsers import StrOutputParser # Initialize the language model llm = ChatOpenAI(temperature=0) # --- Prompt 1: Extract information --- prompt_extract = ChatPromptTemplate.from_template( "Extract the technical specifications from " "the following text:\n\n{text_input}" ) # --- Prompt 2: Transform to JSON --- prompt_transform = ChatPromptTemplate.from_template( "Transform the following specifications into " "a JSON object with 'cpu', 'memory', and " "'storage' as keys:\n\n{specifications}" ) # --- Build the chain using LCEL --- extraction_chain = prompt_extract | llm | StrOutputParser() full_chain = ( {"specifications": extraction_chain} | prompt_transform | llm | StrOutputParser() ) # --- Run the chain --- input_text = "The new laptop model features a 3.5 GHz " "octa-core processor, 16GB of RAM, " "and a 1TB NVMe SSD." final_result = full_chain.invoke({"text_input": input_text}) print(final_result)

Code Walkthrough

extraction_chain runs the first prompt and parses the output to a string. full_chain takes that string, injects it into the second prompt's {specifications} variable, runs the LLM, and returns final JSON. LangChain Expression Language (LCEL) uses the pipe operator | to elegantly connect these components. Each step is independent, testable, and replaceable. langchain-openai can be substituted with other provider packages (e.g., Google Gemini or Anthropic).

Alternative: LangGraph for stateful chains

from langgraph.graph import StateGraph, END from typing import TypedDict class ChainState(TypedDict): text_input: str specifications: str json_output: str def extract_step(state: ChainState) -> ChainState: # Run extraction prompt against LLM result = extraction_chain.invoke({"text_input": state["text_input"]}) return {**state, "specifications": result} def transform_step(state: ChainState) -> ChainState: # Run transformation prompt against LLM result = (prompt_transform | llm | StrOutputParser()).invoke( {"specifications": state["specifications"]} ) return {**state, "json_output": result} graph = StateGraph(ChainState) graph.add_node("extract", extract_step) graph.add_node("transform", transform_step) graph.set_entry_point("extract") graph.add_edge("extract", "transform") graph.add_edge("transform", END) app = graph.compile()
Context Engineering
Context Engineering is the systematic discipline of designing, constructing, and delivering a complete informational environment to an AI model prior to token generation. It represents a significant evolution from traditional prompt engineering. Click each layer to explore:
System Prompt
The foundational instruction set defining the AI's operational parameters
Defines the AI's role, tone, and constraints. For example: "You are a technical documentation writer; your tone must be formal and precise." This foundational layer shapes all subsequent behavior and establishes the framework and boundaries for the entire interaction.
Retrieved Documents
External information actively fetched from a knowledge base
The AI actively fetches information from a knowledge base to enrich its response — for example, pulling the technical specifications for a project. This extends the context far beyond the user's immediate query, ensuring responses are grounded in the most current and relevant information available.
Tool Outputs
Real-time data provided by external APIs and tools
Results from external APIs — querying a calendar for availability, checking a database, calling a search engine. These provide real-time, up-to-date information that the model cannot know from training data alone. Tool outputs enable the AI to interact with real-world systems and produce grounded responses.
Implicit Data
User identity, interaction history, environmental state
A context-engineered agent doesn't just answer a query — it first integrates the user's calendar availability (tool output), professional relationship with an email recipient (implicit data), and notes from previous meetings (retrieved documents), producing output that is highly relevant, personalized, and pragmatically useful.
Feedback Loops
Runtime continuous improvement of context quality
Creating robust pipelines to fetch and transform data at runtime, and establishing feedback loops to continually improve context quality. For example, Google's Vertex AI Prompt Optimizer can enhance model performance by systematically evaluating responses against predefined metrics, adapting prompts and system instructions across models without extensive manual rewriting.

Core Principle

Even advanced models underperform when provided with a limited or poorly constructed view of the operational environment. Context Engineering reframes the task from "answer a question" to "build a comprehensive operational picture for the agent." It treats context itself as the primary component, focusing on what the agent knows, when it knows it, and how it uses that information. Ultimately, it is the key methodology for advancing stateless chatbots into highly capable, situationally-aware systems.

At a Glance

THE PROBLEM

Complex tasks overwhelm LLMs when handled within a single prompt, causing instruction neglect, contextual drift, error propagation, and hallucination. Monolithic prompts struggle to manage multiple constraints and multi-step reasoning.

THE SOLUTION

Prompt chaining breaks complex problems into a series of smaller, interconnected sub-tasks. Each step uses a focused prompt to perform a specific operation, significantly improving reliability and control through a modular divide-and-conquer strategy.

THE MECHANISM

The output of one LLM call becomes the input for the next, creating a dependency chain. Structured output formats (JSON, XML) between steps ensure clean, unambiguous data handoffs. External tools and APIs can be integrated at any step.

WHEN TO USE

Use when: the task is too complex for a single prompt; it involves multiple distinct processing phases; it requires interaction with external tools between steps; or you are building agents that need multi-step reasoning and state management.

Rule of Thumb

If you find yourself writing a single prompt that asks the model to do more than 2-3 distinct things, it is almost always better to decompose it into a chain. The slight overhead in code complexity is paid back many times over in reliability, debuggability, and output quality.

Key Takeaways
  • Prompt chaining (the Pipeline Pattern) decomposes complex tasks into a series of smaller, focused steps — each step has a single responsibility and clear scope.
  • Each step involves one LLM call or processing operation, using the previous step's output as input, creating a dependency chain that progressively builds toward a solution.
  • This pattern significantly improves the reliability, debuggability, and manageability of complex LLM interactions by making each step simpler and less ambiguous.
  • Using structured output formats (JSON, XML) between steps is critical — it ensures clean, unambiguous data handoffs and is the cornerstone of chain reliability.
  • Assigning distinct roles to each step (e.g., "Market Analyst," "Trade Analyst") is a design pattern that focuses the model's output and improves domain-appropriate accuracy.
  • Frameworks including LangChain, LangGraph, Crew AI, and Google ADK provide robust tooling for defining, managing, and executing these multi-step sequences.
  • Context Engineering — building a rich informational environment beyond just the prompt — is the next evolution, treating context itself as the primary design component.
  • Prompt chaining is the foundational pattern that enables the development of advanced AI agents capable of multi-step reasoning, tool integration, and state management.

Summary

By deconstructing complex problems into a series of simpler, more manageable sub-tasks, prompt chaining provides a robust framework for directing large language models. This divide-and-conquer strategy significantly enhances output reliability and control by focusing the model on one specific operation at a time. As a foundational pattern, it enables the development of advanced AI agents capable of multi-step reasoning, tool integration, and state management — well beyond the capability of any single prompt.
